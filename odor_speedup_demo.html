Background¶

Flies and other insects have been observed to use a "cast and surge" behavior to chase an odor plume to its source .

To understand and simulate plume-tracking behavior, it is useful to have an efficient method to compute the odor experienced by a swarm of flies as they move through the odor produced by time-evolving odor plumes.

We borrow Matt Graham's time-evolving plume simulation, which uses the puff-based plume approximation developed by Farrell et al 2002.

Say we have $M$ fly locations, and for each fly-location, we want to compute the experienced odor concentration, which is the sum of the values of $N$ 2D Gaussian concentration distributions.

Speedup with Grid Approximation¶

In Matt's original implementation, this computation takes order $M*N$, which grows slow with a large ensemble of flies. We can use a grid-based method (inspired from the techniques used here) to speed up the fly-odor computation to order $M+N$ (implementation here).

The rough idea is split space into a grid, and to sum for each fly the contributions only from the puffs that are in grid boxes near the fly.

Theory of the Approximation

We borrow from some of the techniques discussed here to implement the approximation.

Instead of computing the concentration for every source for every target (order $M*N$), we instead

Form a grid of width $R$ on the xy space.

Assign each target (fly) to its box. (order $M$)

Assign each source (plume puff) to its box. (order $N$)

Loop through each box: For every target in the box, sum the contributions from each source in that box AND in the eight surrounding boxes.

To choose the appropriate size of $R$: We want $R$ to be small enough that in the worst cast scenario, if all the sources ($\leq N$) that were not within those 9 boxes, were all right against the edge so that they were $R$ away from a given target, the summed contribution of those not-counted sources would be insignificant, or less than a desired $\epsilon$--for the case of the 3D Gaussian puffs in pompy, we have (for unit concentration per puff), this is to require

$$ N \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma^3\sqrt{8\pi^3}} \exp{\Big[-\frac{R^2}{2\sigma^2}\Big]} < \epsilon $$

where $\sigma$ corresponds to the widest possible source. or equivalently,

$$ R^2 \geq -\log\Big[\epsilon\frac{\sqrt{8\pi^3}}{N}\cdot \sigma^3\Big]\cdot 2\sigma^2 $$
